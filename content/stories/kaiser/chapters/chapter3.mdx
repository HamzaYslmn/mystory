{/* Beats as flowing prose in the grave voice. The debt comes due. Kaiser never knew about it —
    proud Izzet hid it (Ch2) — so he pieces it together in the moment, watching. Izzet is hurt
    first: that is the trigger. Kaiser has never used his hands as weapons; this one act is against
    everything he is, and it goes as badly as violence goes for a man who does not do it. His death,
    earned by the bond built in Ch2. Ends on the pull into the Mist. */}

1. One night after dark, three men come in out of the rain, and they move like men who already own
   the place — spreading out, taking the room, one of them settling himself between Izzet and the
   door. Kaiser is at the counter with his own parts laid out in front of him. He does not have the
   details yet. But the moment he sees Izzet go still and gray, it comes together in him all at once:
   this is the thing the old man has been hiding. The quiet afternoon visitors, the ledger closed
   over whenever he looked, the way Izzet flinched at the sound of the door — all of it was leading
   here, and tonight it has come due.

2. Kaiser reads the three of them in a glance. The youngest — Cengo, the others call him — is all
   nerves and eager to be seen. The big one beside him is calm the way a man goes calm when he has
   done this a hundred times. The third, middle-aged, a long scar down one cheek, is no one
   important: a collector sent to lean on a debtor. But he is the one who talks, and he never has to
   raise his voice.

3. The rest Kaiser gathers from the way it goes. Izzet owes money — the kind a man borrows from
   people like these when there is nowhere left to turn, and the kind that is not allowed to go
   unpaid. Whatever time he bought himself has run out. The scarred man lays it out plainly: tonight,
   and all of it. Izzet argues back with more pride than fear, and he drags it out too long.

4. At a nod from the scarred man, the big one steps in and puts Izzet on the floor — one punch, then
   the butt of a pistol across his face — and leaves him there, bleeding, too hurt to rise. To them
   it is nothing, a warning, a night's work. To Kaiser it is the one thing in the world that can
   break him.

5. All his life Kaiser's hands have built things and mended them; they have never once been raised
   against a man. But he sees the blood on the floor and the old man not moving, and the thinking
   simply stops. He does not decide to move — he is already moving. The heavy wrench is off the
   counter and out of his hand before he knows it is gone, thrown with everything in him, and it
   cracks into the side of Cengo's head and drops him. The pistol skids away somewhere under a shelf.

6. Then it comes apart all at once, the way it always does for a man who has never done this before.
   The big one slams Kaiser back into the shelves. The scarred man is shouting for everyone to hold
   still. But Cengo drags himself up with blood on his face and murder in his eyes, finds the gun,
   and fires at the one who made him look small in front of the others. The shot takes Kaiser high in
   the chest and puts him down on the floor beside Izzet.

7. For a moment no one moves. Then the scarred man rounds on the boy, furious — they came to
   frighten an old man, not to leave a body on the floor. A gunshot trips the sensors within seconds;
   the patrol drones are already coming. The debt is forgotten. The big one hauls the dazed Cengo up
   and out into the rain, the scarred man empties the register on his way past, and the three of them
   run — because a killing, and the drones a killing brings, is the one thing a man fresh out of
   prison cannot afford. A beaten old man who lives is not worth the trouble of silencing.

8. Izzet drags himself across the floor, both bloody hands pressed to the wound, shouting for a help
   that will not come in this part of the city. Kaiser barely hears him. All his life he kept his
   head down and his hands to himself, and it kept him alive, and it kept him empty. The one time he
   reached into the world instead of watching it go by, it has cost him everything. And still —
   looking up at the old man's beaten face, alive, here, saying his name — he would not take it back.
   For once in his life he mattered to someone. Kaiser smiles. Izzet is alive.

9. The cold rises through the floor and into him, and the pain loosens its grip and drifts away.
   Izzet's voice grows faint and far off. The light of the shop narrows to a single point, and then
   it goes out. But death — that complete, weightless silence — is not the nothing he always took it
   for. Somewhere far down in the dark, something is waiting for him.
