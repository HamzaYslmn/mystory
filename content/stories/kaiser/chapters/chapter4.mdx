{/* Outline. Turkish prose later. The philosophical set piece of Arc 1. Register: mythic and grave,
   — grand but plain, never purple. Death is a gray mist-realm that
    unmakes and recycles the dead (indifference, not hate). Kaiser does NOT surrender: he fights to
    hold himself, rebuilding lost memories from what remains — and in the fighting he finds the mist
    can strip the memories but not the method doing the rebuilding. That process is what survives and
    reboots him. Let every sentence flow; don't repeat an image once it has landed. */}

1. The first thing to reach him is the cold — deep, bone-deep, the cold of a place that has never once
   been warm. His body is still on the shop floor, bleeding out, but his mind has already gone
   somewhere else: down into an endless gray with no edges to it and no hour. Death, he finds, does not
   feel like an ending. It feels like a place — somewhere he has been carried and set down, on purpose.

2. He does not know where he is or what is being done to him, so he falls back on the one thing he has
   always done in front of something he doesn't understand: he watches. Pale shapes drift past him on
   the same slow current, the way he is drifting. Most go quietly — the ones who arrive carrying
   nothing loosen and thin and let the gray take them, the way a sleeper lets go of the day. Others do
   not go quietly at all; the mist closes on them and pulls, and they come apart in long screaming
   layers. The whispering he took for wind is the sound of minds ending. Whatever a soul will not put
   down on its own, this place takes by force.

3. Watch a thing long enough and it shows you what it is. This is no afterlife. Nothing here weighs his
   life or waits to judge it. It is a process — patient, tireless, working the same way on every soul
   that drifts in: it takes each one apart, keeps whatever can still be used, and lets the rest go.
   Whatever people pictured waiting on the far side of death, this is what is actually there — a
   purgatory with no heaven behind it, only the quiet work of unmaking. What unsettles him most is not
   despair; it is the lack of it. The place holds no anger toward him and no interest in him at all. To
   something like this, a person is nothing sacred — only a pattern it has not cleaned up yet.

4. Then it reaches him. The edges of his hands blur and lift, and the mist works inward, and one by one
   it begins pulling his memories loose. The pain goes first. Then a name. Then a face he knows he
   loved, though the knowing outlasts the face. Then the city he came from — each one thinning and
   rising off him like smoke off a snuffed wick.

5. And here the place makes its mistake, because Kaiser has never once been a man who lets go of a thing
   while he can still get a hand on it. His first instinct is not to grieve what is leaving — it is to
   hold it. He grabs at the memories as they lift, and where one is already gone he does what he has
   always done with a system missing a part: he rebuilds it from what remains, reasons the lost piece
   back out of the pieces still in his hands. A face from a voice. A name from a habit. The harder the
   mist strips him down, the harder he works to put himself back, patient and stubborn against a current
   the size of a universe — not because he believes he can win, but because he means to understand the
   thing that is killing him, and because he will not simply go out.

6. He is still losing. Every fragment he saves costs him the strength to save the next, and the source
   he rebuilds from keeps shrinking under his hands. But somewhere in the fight — patching himself
   together for the hundredth time — he catches sight of the thing he has been overlooking the whole
   while. It is not the memories that keep coming back. It is the rebuilding. Take the name, the face,
   the city, and something is still there: still reaching, still working the problem. A self is not what
   a mind holds — it is the shape of the mind, the way it turns, over and over, toward the pattern in
   things. You cannot burn that out of a man by emptying him, because it was never a thing kept anywhere
   to be found. It is only ever happening. And it is exactly this that the mist keeps closing on and
   coming away with nothing.

7. And it notices. It exists to unmake, and here is a fragment remaking itself faster than it can be
   undone — an error the cycle cannot leave alone. So it stops merely wearing at him and turns its whole
   weight onto that one stubborn mind. His thoughts crack like a sky splitting open. He is pressed
   smaller and smaller, every last scrap of him forced together, until nothing is left of Kaiser but a
   single dense and burning point.

8. But press a mind hard enough and it does not go out — it catches. In the instant he is certain there
   is nothing left, that last point flares white and violent in the dark. Not a memory. Not a name. Only
   the pure, unkillable habit of being a mind. The silence of death breaks all at once into noise and
   the roar of heavy rain, and somewhere far below, in a world he has never seen, a dead man draws a
   breath that tastes of earth and blood — and his eyes snap open.
