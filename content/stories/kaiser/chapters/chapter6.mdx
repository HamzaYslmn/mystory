title: "Chapter 6 — The Dead Component"

{/* Beats as flowing prose in the grave voice. Daylight: he finally examines the glow — a mechanism,
    and a dead one, worked out gradually. Dim ≠ dead: the hypothesis that drives him. Worth-inversion
    payoff at the end. */}

1. He wakes to a gray morning, weak and stiff and — to his own surprise — still alive. There is no
   medicine of any kind, so he cleans the wound the only way he can: rinses it with rainwater and
   binds it fresh. Among the spilled cargo he finds some strange purple roots and eats them, and
   learns the hard way to go slowly, when too many of them turn his stomach.

2. In daylight he goes back to the thing that glowed in the night. The blue has dimmed to a faint
   pulse now. It comes from a small six-sided crystal set into the hub of one wheel, and the thin
   lines that run from it into the wood have gone dry and gray, like ink left to harden. He follows
   the way they run — out from the crystal, along the spokes — and begins to suspect they were never
   meant as decoration. Inside the crystal's glassy body, finer lines catch the light: a deliberate
   pattern, not a flaw in the stone. This was built. It did something, once, and whatever it did, it
   has stopped doing it. He does not know how that is possible in a world like this. But a broken
   mechanism is precisely the kind of thing he understands — and for the first time since he died,
   that might be worth something.

3. He goes through the rest of the wreck the way a man takes inventory, not the way a man loots: a
   hunting knife, a waterproof bag, some charcoal, lengths of tent cloth. Tools and materials for
   later, not only what he needs tonight.

4. He works the faint crystal free of the hub, and it comes away whole and cold, still holding its
   dim pulse. A crystal in another wheel has gone dark — its body already black and brittle — and at
   the first touch of his knife it crumbles to dust. The contrast gives him his first real conclusion
   about this world: a crystal's charge and a crystal's condition are two different things. His is
   only dim. It is not ruined — run down, perhaps, but a long way from dead.

5. He tries to wake it the way he would test any part he did not understand — presses it to wood, to
   stone, to steel; tries pressure, friction, the warmth of his own hands. Half-dead in the night, he
   could have sworn it stirred for him. Now, whatever he tries, nothing answers. He does not reach
   for a magical explanation. He decides instead that it needs some input he has not found yet, and
   pockets it — a locked problem, waiting on the key.

6. For the first time, he understands what it is he is holding. He cannot remember where he came
   from, or the people who were there; the mist took all of it. But the shape of him is still here —
   the same restless need to take a thing apart and see how it works, the need nobody in his old life
   ever had a use for. Now he holds an impossible machine, in a world that seems to be built out of
   them: a world that might, at last, have a use for him. He does not know what killed the people in
   this wagon. He does not know what may be watching from the trees while a wounded stranger picks
   through their remains. He knows only this — that he is going to take this world apart until he
   understands it, or die in the trying. And after everything the last world did to him, that almost
   feels like a fair trade.
