1. There is one place in Anakara that Kaiser can stand to be, and it is the repair shop on the ground
   floor of his own building. Old Izzet runs it — he fixes what the city throws out and sells on what
   he can. It is cramped and warm and it smells of oil and hot metal. Most evenings Kaiser comes down
   with a box of dead parts to trade, and for a few hours the cold of the room upstairs lets go of
   him.

2. Izzet took him on years ago. Kaiser was a boy then who could feel what was wrong with a machine
   the moment he laid a hand on it, and who tore into it half-guessing all the same, and who got
   cheated on every job because he never once knew what his own work was worth. Izzet found him one
   afternoon fixing a neighbor's heater for the price of a meal. He watched the boy's hands a while,
   then said the work was sloppy but the eye behind it was rare, and told him to come back when he
   was ready to learn to do it properly.

3. Izzet was a master mechanic from the years when the city still built things instead of only
   breaking them. He was hard to please and never satisfied, and he did not once say good. He made
   Kaiser strip and rebuild the same gearbox three times over — too fast, then too rough, then at
   last right — and when it was right he only grunted and turned to the next thing. For the whole of
   that first year, Kaiser hated him a little.

4. What the old man was really teaching was not the work but how to think about it. Do not guess. Do
   not fall in love with your first idea of what is wrong. Make the machine show you before you lay a
   hand on it. Every time Kaiser reached for a part too soon, Izzet knocked his hand aside with the
   same question: "What's it doing? Not what you think — what is it actually doing?" He asked it until
   it wore a groove in the boy, until it became the way his mind moved.

5. Talent became skill. The jobs that used to beat him he now opens up clean; the ones Izzet used to
   take back and redo, he no longer gets wrong. The old man never says so. But one evening he slides
   the hardest job on the bench across to Kaiser without a word and turns to something else, and
   Kaiser understands exactly what that means, and says nothing either.

6. Now and then Izzet takes him to the Pas Çarşısı — the city's great scrap market, miles of stalls
   and gutted machines, salvage weighed out by the ton. He teaches Kaiser to read it: what carries
   real worth, what is junk dressed up to pass for more, how to haggle, how to find the one good part
   in a heap of dead ones. They eat cheap from a stall, and Izzet talks — of the Anakara that used to
   run, of the trades that died one after another, of a family he names once and never again. Kaiser
   trusts machines and almost no people. Izzet is the exception. Neither of them ever says aloud what
   they are to each other; there is no need. It is simply the one place in the whole city where
   Kaiser is seen.

7. Kaiser has never kept track of his own birthday; there was never a reason to. So it stops him in
   the doorway one evening to find the workbench cleared and food laid out on it. Not the state's
   tasteless capsules — a real meal, cooked, still warm, the kind of food he has only ever seen
   behind tower glass. Izzet is gruff about it and will not meet his eye, and mutters that a man
   should eat something with a taste to it once a year. Kaiser cannot find anything to say. Nothing
   the world has ever done to him has landed as hard as this.

8. And it was never the once. He sees it now, all of it at the same time: the coat that happened to
   be his size the winter his own fell apart; the parts Izzet overpaid for on the nights the boy
   plainly had not eaten; the heater left burning long past closing whenever Kaiser stayed late. The
   hard hand and the quiet kindness were the same hand. He is only now old enough to know it.

9. Lately, though, something is wrong in the shop. The paying work has dried up — the whole district
   squeezed until there is nothing left to squeeze — and there is a money trouble the old man will
   not name. A ledger he closes over when Kaiser looks at it. Quiet men who come in the afternoons
   and leave the moment the boy walks in. The way Izzet goes still at the sound of the door. Whatever
   it is, he is too proud to set it on anyone else; he means to carry it and settle it himself, and
   every time Kaiser asks he waves it off. It is nothing. It is handled. Kaiser feels the wrongness
   and lets it lie. He can read any machine in the city and he cannot read the man in front of him —
   and that, this once, is going to cost him.
