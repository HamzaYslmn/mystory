{/* Beats written as flowing prose in the grave, plain voice (see .claude/CLAUDE.md and
    docs/voice-study.md). Portrait chapter, no crystals — Kaiser shown through what he does.
    Refrain: every broken thing wears a fix he can see and is not allowed to touch; it turns outward
    in the final beat. Boiler beyond repair, crude workaround. Cyberpunk, filthy, decayed. */}

1. The country is called the Redland, and its capital is Anakara, and both are run the same way: by
   the few at the top, off the backs of everyone below. The rain that falls on it is acid, and the
   streets it falls on are gray. Far overhead, the towers of the rich glow warm against the clouds.
   Kaiser lives at the bottom of all of it, in one rented room of an old three-bedroom flat — a
   different tenant behind each door, the kitchen and the front room shared out among strangers. The
   room holds only what is his, and what is his is not much.

2. What he has, he made. The room is a workshop. In one corner a heap of dead electronics and scrap
   rises against the wall; along another, half-built projects wait in the order he abandoned them. By
   the window stand two salvaged screens and a computer he built himself, its boards packed into a
   case of folded cardboard. Beside it sits a cutting machine he built to carve his own circuit
   boards, and a printer that presses out the parts he cannot scavenge. Almost nothing here came from
   a shop. Everything in the room that works, he made to work.

3. Across the hall lives an old woman, one of the few people in the city who treats him like
   something other than a stranger. She tells him the old stories, the ones from before, and now and
   then she folds a few coins into his hand and will not take them back. One evening he hears her
   tell the others that the cold has settled into her chest. He goes down to the cellar to look at
   the boiler. It died last winter; the mild months made it easy to put off, and putting off became
   forgetting. A note is taped to its side in a repairman's hand: *Pressure regulator damaged. Cannot
   be repaired.* The regulator is the part that holds the pressure steady; without it, the boiler will not
   run at all. The one true fix is the original part. That part stopped being made long ago, and a
   single one would cost more than the whole building earns in a year.

4. He cannot replace the regulator. He does not need to replace it — he needs one winter. So he
   makes the dead part's work get done another way. He forces a length of pipe into the
   pressure-relief valve and bends it until it holds a fixed gap, so the boiler bleeds off whatever
   it builds instead of letting the pressure climb. It is crude, and it is wrong: it leaks steam, it
   burns more fuel than it should. But the pressure sits low and steady, and heat moves back through
   the pipes of the building. "This should get us through another winter," he says, to no one, and
   goes out into the rain.

5. The food machine that serves his block sits at the far edge of it, an hour away on foot, and the
   city comes at him the whole way, one thing at a time. He turns his collar up against the acid rain
   and keeps to the overhangs that still hold. His own street first: shops shuttered for good, a dead
   streetlight he could bring back in ten minutes and never will, an autocab stripped to its shell
   and up on bricks while children pull the last of it apart. Then the bridge over the drainage
   canal, its joints red with rust, the deck sagging under a passing truck — and his eye goes
   straight to the one cracked weld carrying the load, and to the winter it will finally give. The
   market strip: dead neon over closed shopfronts, and above it a bright, cheerful holo-ad selling
   tower food to a crowd that will never taste any of it. The drains have overflowed, and he steps
   around the filth in the gutter without looking down. An alley choked with trash, and a man lying
   in it, asleep or dead, and no one stopping to learn which. A patrol drone sweeps low, and the
   whole street goes still until it passes. A checkpoint that exists for no reason but to make the
   poor stand longer in the wet; he shows his card, waits, moves on. Everywhere he looks, something
   is failing, and every failure wears a fix he can see and is not allowed to touch. Then, at the end
   of it, the line.

6. The line is long, and longer every week. The portions shrink, and the people standing in it have
   begun to believe the machines are running dry. They are not. The machines are full; the rations
   are skimmed off the top by the administrators who run them, sold on, and the shortfall handed down
   to the street as hunger. Fear does the rest. Emboldened by it, a knot of thugs shoulders up the
   line, knocks the old man at its head down into the mud, and takes his place. A police officer
   watches it happen and does nothing. He answers to money, and there is none here. The law in
   Anakara is one more machine that works only for those who can pay it.

7. He reaches the machine near the end, as he almost always does — his block is the farthest out,
   and there are only six or seven left behind him now, the muddied old man among them. It gives him
   his ration: a handful of tasteless capsules, a day of nutrition and nothing more. He knows this
   model down to its wiring; he has pulled a dozen of them apart for scrap. The portions come up
   short because a weight sensor has been set to read the tray as heavier than it is. The two fuses
   that feed that sensor sit in a box on the right of the housing. He slips them out in a single
   motion, and the scale goes blind, and the machine — reading the tray as empty — pours out three
   times what it should. The few people behind him leave with their arms full and their faces bright,
   not knowing why. The attendant sees the joy and knows something is wrong and cannot find what; he
   drives them off, and in the scuffle Kaiser is already gone.

8. Back in his room he lays out the ration: magnesium, protein tablets, hard carb bars, a day's
   nutrition pressed into compounds with no salt and no sweetness and no taste. He has learned to
   make one thing of it that he almost likes — crumb-loaf, he calls it: the biscuit-hard bars crushed
   into water and boiled down to a dough that sets somewhere between bread and cake. While it cooks,
   he lets the news play. The government is clearing the hospitals of the ones with Sarpic; the
   streets have filled with the sick, and the city has grown tired of the sight of them. The rest of
   it runs the same, ordinary and rotten in the same breath: an administrator honored for efficiency
   savings in the very week the rations shrank; water rationed in the lower wards while the towers
   keep their fountains running; a prison quietly emptied to cut its costs, the freed men turned
   loose into streets like his.

9. Out of habit, before he sleeps, he designs the fix. A way to close the very hole he used
   tonight — a few lines of code, a better sensor that cannot be told to lie. He saves it into a
   folder that already holds hundreds like it, every one a real answer to a real problem, every one
   worth nothing. No one buys an idea that comes up out of the Undercity. Because the idea is his, it
   is worthless — not because it is wrong.

10. He shuts the computer down and lets the cold come back into the room, shallower now that the
    boiler runs. Tonight he and the old woman across the hall will sleep a little warmer, and that is
    the whole of what he has changed. He lies down and looks out at the warm lights of the towers, a
    world close enough to see and sealed against him. And he wonders, the way he does most nights,
    whether somewhere there is a different world — one where knowing how a thing works is worth
    something. Where an idea is heard for what it is, and not for whose hands it came out of. Where
    fixing the broken thing in front of you means anything at all.
