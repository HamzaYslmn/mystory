title: "Chapter 5 — Awakening"

{/* Beats as flowing prose in the grave voice. Dazed, physical awakening; memory fog (personal
    memories gone, method intact); only a glimpse of the blue glow at the end. The reasoning waits
    for the next chapter's daylight. */}

1. First there is only the rain — hard, cold, falling out of a sky he cannot see. The rest comes back
   in pieces that do not fit together: mud against his face, blood in his mouth, cold soaked all the
   way through him. His mind still works. That much is whole. But when he reaches back for who he is,
   there is almost nothing there to find. No name to take hold of. No face. Only the certainty that
   there was a life before this, and that something has scraped it away.

2. He tries to move and cannot. Something heavy lies across his back and legs, pressing him down into
   the mud. The first clear thing he feels is not fear but a dull, animal need to be out from under
   it. He works an arm beneath himself and pushes. Wet wood shifts and grinds. He drags himself
   forward a hand's width at a time until the weight slides off him at last, and he comes up gasping,
   on his hands and knees.

3. He lifts his head to make sense of the place and cannot. All around him lies the smashed wreck of
   a great wagon, its load flung out across the mud. There are shapes in the ruin he does not want to
   put a name to — people, more than one, terribly still. Close by, tangled in broken harness, are
   two huge animals he has only ever seen in pictures: horses, dead in the rain. Some far-off part of
   him wonders whether this is only what a dying mind shows itself at the end.

4. But a dying mind does not shiver like this. The cold is real, and it is trying to kill him. He is
   soaked through and shaking, and when he shifts his weight a hot pain drives up his leg — a shard of
   rusted blade buried in his thigh. The where and the how of it can wait. If he
   stays out in this rain, bleeding and freezing, he will not see morning. That much he can still
   work out for himself.

5. He needs to be out of the rain. The overturned wagon is the only thing near enough to put a roof
   between him and the sky. He grits his teeth and starts toward it, half-crawling, dragging the bad
   leg behind him, and every arm's length of it is its own small war. He reaches the wagon with
   nothing left, and leans against the wet planks, and only breathes, the rain hammering on the wood
   above him.

6. When he can move again, he works his way in under the wagon, out of the worst of the rain at
   last. In the cramped quiet he sees to the leg. He takes hold of the broken blade and draws it out
   with a sound he does not recognize as his own, then binds the wound with a strip torn from his
   shirt. It is crude, and it is not enough, but it is something — and his breathing begins, finally,
   to slow.

7. Only then, as his eyes settle into the dark, does he notice the light. It is faint, and it is
   blue, and it comes from the hub of one broken wheel an arm's length from his face — a cold glow
   with no flame behind it, bleeding out into the wet wood in thin lines, like veins. He has no idea
   what it is. He has no strength left to wonder; he is too broken and too cold to do anything at all
   tonight. The last thing he knows, as the dark folds back over him, is that blue light — and the
   half-dreamed sense that, for a moment, it stirs in answer to him.
